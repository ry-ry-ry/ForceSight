# ForceSight — Impeccable Design Context

This file is the source of truth for design work on ForceSight. Every design skill should read it before touching the interface.

## Design Context

### Users

ForceSight has a mixed audience of milsim / wargaming hobbyists, OSINT analysts and defence researchers, and solo hobbyists building fictional force structures. They run the app on desktop, usually for extended sessions, and they already understand military terminology (echelons, rotations, task forces, operations). The interface does not need to teach the domain — it needs to get out of the way and let them record, inspect, and cross-reference dense unit data quickly.

The job to be done: maintain a trustworthy, well-organised record of units, their chain of command, their current/historical deployments, sub-operations and missions, and their effectiveness, and be able to look any of that up in seconds.

### Brand Personality

**Precise · Tactical · Archival.**

The product should feel like a well-crafted reference instrument — closer to a printed military almanac or an intelligence product than to a SaaS dashboard or a video-game HUD. Authoritative and serious. Typography and hierarchy do the heavy lifting; decoration is kept out of the way.

Voice: terse, factual, free of marketing language. Uses military terminology correctly. Timestamps, coordinates, dates, echelon markings should feel load-bearing, not cosmetic.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Single theme, refined.** The `cyber` theme (cyan-on-navy neon) is being **removed** — it reads as generic AI-dashboard and conflicts with the brand. The `army` theme becomes the only theme and is elevated to carry the product's identity alone. The theme toggle is retired.

**Palette direction — keep the accents, rethink the surfaces:**
- **Accents stay.** The brass / khaki / muted-gold palette is on-brand — reminiscent of US Army and Ranger heritage colours. Keep the accent primary in that family.
- **Surfaces get re-grounded.** The current brown/warm-earth page background reads as "leather journal" and drifts generic. Move to a cooler, more neutral foundation: something closer to a stone / gunmetal / cold-olive neutral, with warmth introduced only through the accents and selective typography colour. Think field-manual paper under overhead lighting rather than dark wood panelling.
- **Status colours** (deployed / standby / training / reset) keep their muted, doctrinal tone — no neon.

**Typography direction:**
- Keep **Barlow Condensed** for headings (already right — reads stencilled, official, condensed enough for dense data tables).
- Keep **Inter** for body.
- Keep **JetBrains Mono** strictly for numbers that are actually tabular data — coordinates, dates, timestamps, counts. Not as atmosphere.
- Lean harder into tabular-number features (`font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums`) for any column of numeric data.

**Shape language:**
- Squared-off or barely-rounded corners (2–4px), never pill-shaped.
- Borders are structural, not decorative — hairlines carrying hierarchy. No glow borders, no gradient strokes.
- Cards should be used sparingly; tables, rules, and whitespace carry most of the layout.

### Anti-References (what this must NOT look like)

Explicit failure modes — if the interface starts resembling any of these, pull back:

- **Generic dark-mode SaaS with neon accents.** Cyan-on-navy, purple-to-blue gradients, glowing borders, glassmorphism. This is why `cyber` is being killed.
- **Consumer game UI / arcade HUD.** Chunky bevels, HUD brackets as pure decoration, drop shadows on everything, "MISSION ACCOMPLISHED" energy. Too Call-of-Duty.
- **Cartoonish / playful / Duolingo-esque.** Pastel illustrations, rounded mascots, emoji, bouncy micro-animations. Wrong register for the subject matter.

### Design Principles

1. **Typography is the interface.** Hierarchy, density, and scannability come from type weight, size, and rhythm — not from coloured boxes and icons. Headings use Barlow Condensed stencilled uppercase; tabular numerals are non-negotiable for any numeric column.
2. **Hairlines over containers.** Prefer rules, dividers, and whitespace to bordered cards. Don't wrap every block in a box; don't nest cards. Structure should read like a well-laid-out document, not a grid of widgets.
3. **Accents are signals, not decoration.** The brass/gold accent means "this matters" — status, active state, primary action, selected row. It should never be used purely to add visual interest. If removing the accent wouldn't lose information, the accent shouldn't be there.
4. **Numbers are load-bearing.** Coordinates, rotation-day counts, deployment dates, echelon codes, timestamps: render them with tabular-nums, align them on their decimals/colons, and let them drive layout. A misaligned column of numbers is a worse bug than a misaligned paragraph.
5. **Restraint over spectacle.** One motion per interaction, not five. No ambient pulsing, no shimmer on every button, no background particles. Users open this app to work, not to watch a show.

### Accessibility

- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` everywhere motion is introduced.
- Minimum body text 14px, no text smaller than 10px even for labels.
- Status should never be conveyed by colour alone — always pair with a label or shape.
- No hard requirement to meet a specific WCAG tier right now, but contrast ratios for body text should comfortably clear AA (4.5:1) against the new neutral background.
